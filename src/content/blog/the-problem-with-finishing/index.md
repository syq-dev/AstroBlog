---
title: "The Problem With Finishing"
description: "Why the last 10% of any project is where all the interesting decisions live."
date: "May 13 2026"
---

Every project has two phases: the part where you're figuring out if it works, and the part where you decide what it actually is.

Most people think finishing is about closing out the second phase. It's not. Finishing is when the second phase starts.

The prototype is easy. You're operating in hypothesis mode — nothing is real yet, so nothing is at stake. You can make the button do anything because the button isn't really a button. It's a placeholder for a decision you haven't made.

Then at some point the thing starts working. And suddenly every choice you deferred has to get made for real. The font. The copy. Whether the feature you were most excited about actually belongs in this thing or belongs in a different thing you should probably build separately. The finishing is just a long series of disambiguations you've been putting off since the first commit.

I have a theory that this is why so many side projects stall out at 80%. Not because the builder got lazy. Because the builder hit the point where the project started requiring taste, not just technical decisions — and taste is slower and harder and more exposing than writing code.

You can debug code. You can't really debug taste. You just have to sit with the thing and figure out what it wants to be.

The ones that do get finished? Mostly they finish because someone got annoyed enough to just pick something and ship it. Not because they found the perfect answer. Because they got tired of waiting for it. ⬢
