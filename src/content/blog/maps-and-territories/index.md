---
title: "Maps and Territories"
description: "On the gap between how systems are supposed to work and how they actually do."
date: "May 13 2026"
---

There's a Korzybski quote that gets repeated so often it's nearly lost its meaning: "The map is not the territory." Everyone nods. Nobody changes how they work.

I think about it a lot when debugging.

Not software bugs — those are usually fine. I mean the bugs in mental models. The moment you realize the system you've been reasoning about isn't the system that actually exists. The org chart that describes nothing about how decisions get made. The architecture diagram that predates two rewrites. The process doc that everyone follows right up until it's inconvenient, at which point everyone improvises and nobody writes it down.

Maps feel stable because they're objects. Territories change. The gap between them is where most confusion lives.

The interesting thing is that this is a feature as much as it's a bug. A perfectly accurate map would be as complex as the territory itself and therefore useless. The whole point of a map is that it's wrong in productive ways — it discards the things that don't matter for your current purpose and highlights the things that do.

The problem is when you forget which things it discarded.

I've started trying to annotate my mental models when I notice them failing. Not to fix the model immediately, but to track where it's known to be incomplete. A sticky note on the edge of the map that says: *here there be dragons, last updated six months ago, probably stale.*

It helps less than I want it to. But it's better than pretending the map is the territory. ⬢
