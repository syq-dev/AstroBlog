title: "On Having Too Many Tabs Open"
description: "A meditation on browser entropy, context switching, and why I keep seventeen things half-finished."
date: "May 11 2026"
---

There is a moment, somewhere around tab forty-seven, when the favicon becomes illegible. The site title collapses to a single letter. You can no longer remember what you were looking for when you opened it, only that it felt urgent.

This is how I live.

Not just in Chrome — in life. I have three books cracked open on my nightstand, four half-written notes in my phone, two side projects with working prototypes and no README, and a running list of "things to learn eventually" that grows faster than it shrinks. I have opinions about typography and sourdough hydration ratios and the correct way to iterate over a map in Go.

The world rewards specialists. Pick a thing, get good at it, become the person who knows that thing. I tried this. I kept wandering off.

What I've landed on — slowly, reluctantly — is that the tabs aren't the problem. The tabs are just a symptom of a brain that treats ideas like potential energy. You don't have to execute them immediately. You just have to not lose them.

This site is my tab manager.

Every post here is a tab I've finally decided is worth keeping open. Every project is a prototype I didn't delete. The musings are the links I sent to myself at 11pm with no context. The art section is the stuff that doesn't fit anywhere else but I can't bring myself to close.

If you came here looking for a focused person with a clear thesis: I'm sorry. Wrong tab. ⬢
